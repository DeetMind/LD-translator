Loss Distribution Translator v2

Hail · Residential Property · Roof Upgrade Demo

This tool translates physical risk reduction (e.g., a roof upgrade) into insurance-relevant outcomes under a specified policy structure.

It takes an event-based loss distribution (e.g., hail scenarios), applies mitigation assumptions, and maps resulting losses through a policy layer to show:

  • Changes in expected annual loss (EAL)
  • Changes in insured vs. uninsured loss
  • Where avoided losses sit relative to the deductible and coverage
  • How mitigation impacts decision-relevant insurance metrics

This demo focuses on a single residential property exposed to hail risk. All assumptions (hazard, loss, policy, and mitigation) are user-editable and intended as illustrative starting points only.

1. Where do the input data come from, and what makes them defensible?

The tool itself does not generate loss data. It operates on an event-level loss distribution provided by the user.

Defensibility depends entirely on the source of that distribution. In practice, this could come from:

  • Catastrophe models (e.g., RMS, AIR)
  • Engineering-based studies
  • Insurer or reinsurer loss data
  • Validated public datasets

The current sample data are illustrative and intended only to demonstrate structure.

2. What does the tool actually do?

The tool translates mitigation into decision-relevant insurance outcomes. It:

  • Applies mitigation assumptions to an event-level loss distribution
  • Recomputes expected annual loss (EAL) and exceedance probability (EP) curves
  • Maps losses through a policy structure (deductible, limit)
  • Reports changes in gross, insured, and uninsured loss

It does not model hazard or damage directly.

3. How is mitigation represented?

Mitigation is modeled using three parameters:

  • Base loss reduction
  • Probability of failure
  • Maintenance degradation

These combine into an effective reduction applied to event losses.

This is a simplified representation. It does not model component-level damage, installation variability, or hazard-specific performance curves. These parameters are intended to be replaced with better empirical or engineering-based estimates where available.

4. Does the tool include vulnerability or damage functions?

No. Losses are provided directly in the input CSV. The tool does not convert hazard intensity (e.g., hail size) into damage.

This keeps the tool modular, but means all hazard-to-loss assumptions sit upstream, and the tool cannot validate those assumptions internally.

5. How does the insurance layer work?

The tool applies a simplified policy structure: deductible (flat or % of coverage), coverage limit, and an optional insured share mode.

It then calculates insured loss and uninsured (retained) loss. This allows comparison between physical loss reduction and insurance-relevant outcomes.

6. Why might mitigation reduce gross loss but not insured loss?

Because of policy structure. If avoided losses occur below the deductible or above the coverage limit, then insured loss may change less than gross loss.

This is a central use case of the tool: identifying where mitigation sits relative to the policy layer.

7. Is this asset-level or portfolio-level?

This version is asset-level only. It operates on one asset, one event-loss distribution, and one mitigation profile. It does not currently support portfolios, spatial correlation, or multiple assets or interventions.

8. Can this be validated against real-world outcomes?

Not within the tool itself. Validation would require comparing input loss distributions to observed claims and benchmarking mitigation effects against empirical studies.

At present, outputs are mechanically consistent but depend entirely on input assumptions.

9. What is hazard share?

Hazard share represents the portion of total risk attributable to the modeled hazard (hail in this demo). If provided, the tool translates hazard-specific loss reduction into an estimate of total-risk reduction. If not provided, results are hazard-specific only.

10. What are the main limitations of this version?

  • No hazard or damage modeling (losses are exogenous)
  • Simplified mitigation representation
  • Simplified policy structure
  • Single-asset scope
  • No empirical calibration built in

This version is best understood as a translation and sensitivity tool, not a predictive model.
